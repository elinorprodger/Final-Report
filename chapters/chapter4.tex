\subsection{Structural model validation}
\label{sec:res_validation}
%===============================================================

The structural model was validated against manufacturer design tables as described in Section~\ref{sec:validation}. Across all tested configurations, the model correctly predicted passing and failing conditions for deep deck profiles (ComFlor~225, Multideck~60/80/146) and for the ComFlor~51 family, with most computed spans within the manufacturer-tabulated range. The shallow-deck families ComFlor~60 and ComFlor~80 showed a systematic overprediction of permissible span at the longer span range, attributable primarily to the conservative $A_{p,e}$ fallback assumption and differences between Eurocode~4 equations and the empirically-calibrated UK design tables used by the ComFlor software.

\subsection{Minimum-GWP composite slab: optimal cross-sections}
\label{sec:res_optimal_gwp}
%===============================================================

\subsubsection*{Overview}
The bounded scalar optimisation was performed independently for each combination of deck product (Tata Steel ComFlor and Kingspan Multideck ranges), concrete grade (C25/30 and C30/37), span configuration (single, two-span, three-span continuous), and span length (3--10\,m). The global minimum across all deck products and concrete grades is reported for each case. Propped construction is preferred by the optimiser at longer spans where unpropped sections become unfeasible; unpropped construction is feasible and preferred at shorter spans where the construction-stage checks do not govern.

\subsubsection*{Office scenario --- single span}
\label{sec:res_office}

Figure~\ref{fig:gwp_office_ss} shows the minimum total GWP as a function of span for the \textbf{office} occupancy scenario (single-span configuration), comparing the optimal composite slab against the reinforced concrete flat slab and ribbed slab baselines.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{figures/office/ENV/plot_office_1span_ENV_gwp.png}
    \caption{Minimum total GWP per metre of span length for the office occupancy scenario (single-span configuration), comparing RC flat slab, RC ribbed slab, and composite slab systems under the envelope (ENV) criterion. Points are labelled with the governing limit state.}
    \label{fig:gwp_office_ss}
\end{figure}

Key observations for the office single-span case:
\begin{itemize}
    \item At short spans (3--4\,m), the fire check D1 (thermal insulation, formula D.1) governs for several deck products, setting a minimum required concrete depth above the ribs that exceeds the structurally required depth. The minimum slab depth is therefore set by the fire insulation requirement rather than by structural capacity.
    \item At intermediate spans (5--7\,m), longitudinal shear (m--k method) and fire checks D2 or D4 are the most common governing checks for the products that appear in the optimal set.
    \item At longer spans (8--10\,m), SLS total deflection ($\delta_\mathrm{tot} \le L/300$) governs the design, requiring progressively deeper sections to satisfy the stiffness requirement.
    \item Propped construction is selected by the optimiser for the majority of configurations at spans $\ge 7$\,m. At shorter spans, unpropped construction is generally feasible and preferred, since the construction-stage SLS deflection check is satisfied within the range of available slab depths.
    \item Concrete grade C25/30 is preferred over C30/37 in all observed optimal solutions at this scenario, consistent with the argument that composite slabs at office loads are rarely concrete-strength-governed.
\end{itemize}

At $L = 6$\,m (Multideck~146 1.2\,mm, $h = 212$\,mm, unpropped), the structural GWP is approximately 74\,kg\,CO$_2$-eq/m, of which concrete accounts for roughly half, the steel deck for approximately 39\%, and mesh and trough bars for the remainder. For the two-span case, the same general pattern holds: fire checks govern at short spans, SLS deflection governs at long spans. The hogging ULS and fire D3 checks at internal supports are additional constraints that do not apply for the single-span case.

\label{sec:res_govcheck}
%===============================================================

    \label{fig:govcheck}

For the office scenario (single-span), SLS deflection ($\delta_\mathrm{tot} \le L/300$) is the most common governing check, controlling the optimal depth for approximately 56\% of all configurations evaluated. This reflects the fact that composite slabs are stiffness-governed rather than strength-governed for the majority of practical office-floor configurations: the structural demand can often be met at a shallower depth than the deflection limit requires. Fire check D1 (thermal insulation) is the next most frequent governing check at approximately 15\%, particularly prominent at short spans (3--4\,m) where the minimum concrete depth for 60-minute fire insulation exceeds the structurally required depth. Longitudinal shear (m--k method) governs at approximately 12\% of configurations, predominantly at intermediate spans for shallower deck profiles.

The dominance of SLS deflection as the governing constraint has an important implication for the GWP comparison: increasing span primarily requires a deeper (heavier) section to satisfy stiffness rather than strength, and the marginal GWP increase per additional metre of span is driven largely by increased concrete volume rather than by increased deck mass or reinforcement. This means that the rate of GWP increase with span is relatively smooth and predictable for composite slabs, unlike systems where a discrete jump in reinforcement or section size is required at specific spans.

At $L = 6$\,m, concrete accounts for approximately 50\% of the structural GWP, with the steel deck contributing approximately 39\%, mesh reinforcement 3\%, and fire trough bars 7\%. The relatively high deck contribution at medium spans reflects the high GWP intensity per unit mass of structural steel (approximately 1.5--2.0\,kg\,CO$_2$-eq/kg for the profiles in the database) compared to concrete (approximately 0.10--0.15\,kg\,CO$_2$-eq/kg). As span increases and slab depth grows, the concrete contribution increases proportionally while the deck mass per unit area remains approximately constant, so the concrete fraction rises at longer spans.

The fire trough bar contribution (7\% at $L = 6$\,m) is non-trivial, since the bars are present solely to satisfy the fire sagging moment check (D2) and have no structural contribution at ambient temperature. Their contribution increases with fire severity (higher $\psi_\mathrm{fi}$) and with increasing fire period rating.

\subsection{Optimal deck selection}
\label{sec:res_optimal_sections}
%===============================================================

For the office single-span scenario, the optimiser selects shallow Multideck profiles (50 and 60 series, $h_p = 50$--60\,mm) and Multideck~146 ($h_p = 146$\,mm) at short to medium spans, with ComFlor~60 ($h_p = 60$\,mm) appearing at the 7\,m span. At the longest spans (8--10\,m), Multideck~50 ($h_p = 50$\,mm) with propped construction is selected, reflecting the optimiser's preference for a shallower rib when the design is governed by SLS deflection (since the composite stiffness is primarily a function of total slab depth rather than rib height, and a shallow rib allows a larger concrete section for the same total depth). 

Concrete grade C25/30 is preferred over C30/37 in all observed optimal solutions, consistent with the argument that the composite slab at these loads is not concrete-strength-governed: the concrete compression block depth $x_{pl}$ is small relative to $h$ for all configurations evaluated, so increasing concrete strength provides minimal structural benefit while increasing the GWP intensity per unit mass.

\subsection{Summary}
\label{sec:res_summary}
%===============================================================

For the office occupancy scenario, the minimum-GWP composite slab cross-section is governed by SLS deflection across the majority of practical spans, with fire insulation (D1) controlling at short spans (3--4\,m) and longitudinal shear governing for some intermediate-span configurations. Total GWP ranges from approximately 61\,kg\,CO$_2$-eq/m at $L = 3$\,m to approximately 149\,kg\,CO$_2$-eq/m at $L = 10$\,m, including the fixed floor build-up contribution of approximately 19.6\,kg\,CO$_2$-eq/m. Shallow Multideck profiles dominate the optimal deck selection across the span range, with propped construction preferred at longer spans to satisfy the SLS deflection constraint.
