\subsection{Full-floor vibration check: worked example}
\label{app:vibration}
%===============================================================
This appendix presents a complete bay-level vibration check for a representative office floor, following the SCI~P354 simplified method \citep{RefWorks:hicks2009p354}. It is included to demonstrate the full procedure that the slab-only screening check of Section~\ref{sec:vibration_method} approximates, and to quantify the reduction in natural frequency when the slab is supported on flexible beams rather than rigid supports. The calculation is implemented in \texttt{full\_vibration\_check.py}.

\subsubsection*{Bay geometry and inputs}

The worked example uses the minimum-GWP composite slab at a 6 metre two-span office span (ComFlor~60 0.9\,mm gauge, $h = 166 mm$, C25/30, propped construction), placed within a representative 6 metre~$\times$ 9 metre office bay. Secondary beams span 9m at 6m centres; primary beams also span 9m. Realistic composite beam sections are assumed, consistent with the applied loading.

The floor mass per unit area is
\begin{equation}
    m = \frac{\bigl(g_\mathrm{slab} + g_\mathrm{deck} + \mathrm{SDL} + 0.1\,Q_k\bigr) \times 10^3}{g}
      = \frac{(3.22 + 0.10 + 1.11 + 0.25)\times 10^3}{9.81}
      = 476kg/m^2
\end{equation}
where $g_\mathrm{slab} = 24 \times h_\mathrm{conc,avg}/1000 = 24 \times 134/1000 = 3.22 kN/m^2$ uses the profiled average concrete depth $h_\mathrm{conc,avg} = (h - h_p) + V_\mathrm{trough}{\times}1000 = 106 + 28 = 134mm$.

\subsubsection*{Step 1 --- Component stiffnesses and deflections}

\paragraph{Slab stiffness.}
The dynamic second moment of area per unit width is obtained from the SCI~P354 Table~7.2 power-law fit for a trapezoidal deck with $h_p = 60mm$:
\begin{equation}
    I_s = 0.23\,h^{3.7} = 0.23 \times 166^{3.7} = 37.7 \times 10^6 mm^4/m
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    EI_s = E_s\,I_s = 210{,}000 \times 37.7 \times 10^6 = 7.91\ MN/m^2/m
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Composite beam stiffnesses.}
Using the transformed-section method (SCI~P354 \S4.1.4) with effective slab widths $b_\mathrm{eff} = \min(L/4,\,b)$:
\begin{align}
    b_{\mathrm{eff},s} &= \min(9.0/4,\;6.0) = 2.25m \quad \text{(secondary beam)} \\
    b_{\mathrm{eff},p} &= \min(9.0/4,\;9.0) = 2.25m \quad \text{(primary beam)}
\end{align}
The resulting composite stiffnesses are:
\begin{align}
    EI_b &= 243 MN/m^2 \quad \text{(secondary, 457$\times$191$\times$67\,UB composite)} \\
    EI_p &= 359 MN/m^2 \quad \text{(primary, 533$\times$210$\times$82\,UB composite)}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Component deflections.}
The natural frequency of each component is derived from its static deflection under the vibrating mass $m$ using $f = 18/\sqrt{\delta_\mathrm{mm}}$ (SCI~P354 Eq.~4). The secondary beam mode deflection (Table~7.1) combines the bending of the secondary beam and the local slab flexibility:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_\mathrm{sec} = \frac{m\,g\,b}{384}
        \left(\frac{5\,L_y^4}{EI_b} + \frac{b^3}{EI_s}\right)
    = \frac{476 \times 9.81 \times 6.0}{384}
        \left(\frac{5 \times 9.0^4}{243 \times 10^6} + \frac{6.0^3}{7.91 \times 10^6}\right)
    = 11.84 mm
\end{equation}
The primary beam mode deflection (simply-supported beam, UDL $= m\,g\,L_y$):
\begin{equation}
    \delta_\mathrm{prim} = \frac{5\,m\,g\,L_y\,L_x^4}{384\,EI_p}
    = \frac{5 \times 476 \times 9.81 \times 9.0 \times 9.0^4}{384 \times 359 \times 10^6}
    = 10.02mm
\end{equation}
The corresponding component frequencies are:
\begin{align}
    f_\mathrm{slab} &= \frac{18}{\sqrt{9.96}} = 5.70Hz \\
    f_\mathrm{sec}  &= \frac{18}{\sqrt{11.84}} =5.23Hz \\
    f_\mathrm{prim} &= \frac{18}{\sqrt{10.02}} =5.69Hz
\end{align}
Note that $f_\mathrm{slab} = 5.70Hz$ matches the slab-only screening value for the same section and span (Section~\ref{sec:vibration_method}), confirming consistency between the two calculations.

\subsubsection*{Step 2 --- Fundamental floor frequency}

Per SCI~P354 \S7, the governing deflection mode is the larger of the two beam modes:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_0 = \max(\delta_\mathrm{sec},\,\delta_\mathrm{prim}) = \max(11.84,\;10.02) = 11.84mm
    \quad \text{(secondary beam governs)}
\end{equation}
The fundamental frequency of the combined floor system is therefore:
\begin{equation}
    f_0 = \frac{18}{\sqrt{\delta_0}} = \frac{18}{\sqrt{11.84}} = 5.23Hz
\end{equation}
This is 8.3\% below the slab-alone screening frequency of 5.70Hz, confirming that the slab-only check provides a conservative upper bound on the floor frequency. Since $f_0 \geq 3Hz$, the floor is not susceptible to resonance with the third harmonic of walking and the full response-factor check proceeds.

\subsubsection*{Step 3 --- Modal mass and response factor}

Since $5 < f_0 \leq 6Hz$, the $\eta$ factor interpolated from SCI~P354 Table~7.3 is:
\begin{equation}
    \eta = 0.21\,f_0 - 0.55 = 0.21 \times 5.23 - 0.55 = 0.549
\end{equation}

The effective floor length $L_\mathrm{eff}$ and width $S$ mobilised by the mode (SCI~P354 Eqs.~45--46, with $n_{y,\mathrm{eff}} = n_{x,\mathrm{eff}} = 4$):
\begin{align}
    L_\mathrm{eff} &= 1.09 \times 1.10^{(n_y-1)/2}
        \left(\frac{EI_b}{m\,b\,f_0^2}\right)^{0.25} L_y
    = 1.09 \times 1.10^{1.5} \times 7.47 \times 9.0 = 84.6m
    \;\to\; \min(84.6,\;4 \times 9) = 36.0m \\
    S &= \eta \times 1.15^{(n_x-1)/2}
        \left(\frac{EI_s}{m\,f_0^2}\right)^{0.25}
    = 0.549 \times 1.15^{1.5} \times 4.97 = 3.36m
\end{align}
The modal mass is:
\begin{equation}
    M = m\,L_\mathrm{eff}\,S = 476 \times 36.0 \times 3.36 = 57600kg
\end{equation}

The resonance build-up factor (SCI~P354 Eq.~37, walking pace $f_p = 2.0Hz$, speed $v_w = 1.5m/s$, path $L_p = L_y = 9.0m$):
\begin{equation}
    \rho = 1 - \exp\!\left(-\frac{2\pi\,\zeta\,f_p\,L_p}{v_w}\right)
         = 1 - \exp\!\left(-\frac{2\pi \times 0.03 \times 2.0 \times 9.0}{1.5}\right)
         = 1 - e^{-2.26} = 0.896
\end{equation}

Since $f_0 = 5.23Hz \leq 8Hz$, the frequency weighting factor is $W = 1.0$ (flat region of the BS~6472 $W_g$ curve). The floor is classified as low-frequency ($f_0 < 10Hz$) and the RMS acceleration response is computed from SCI~P354 Eq.~50:
\begin{equation}
    a_\mathrm{rms} = \frac{0.1\,Q\,\rho\,W}{\sqrt{2}\,\zeta\,M}
    = \frac{0.1 \times 746 \times 0.896 \times 1.0}{\sqrt{2} \times 0.03 \times 57{,}600}
    = 0.0274m/s^2
\end{equation}
where $Q = 746N$ is the representative pedestrian force (76\,kg). The response factor relative to the BS~6472 base curve ($0.005m/s^2$) is:
\begin{equation}
    R = \frac{a_\mathrm{rms}}{0.005} = \frac{0.0274}{0.005} = 5.47
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{Result}

\begin{equation}
    R = 5.47 \;\leq\; R_\mathrm{lim} = 8.0 \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad
    \text{utilisation} = \frac{5.47}{8.0} = 0.684 \quad \textbf{PASS}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection*{Discussion}

The representative office bay passes the full SCI~P354 response-factor check with a utilisation of 0.68. The combined floor frequency of 5.23Hz is 8.3\% below the slab-alone screening value of 5.70Hz, confirming that the slab-only check is conservative and that the full floor frequency falls below the screened value when realistic beam flexibility is accounted for. The reduction is modest here because the secondary and primary beam deflections (11.84mm and 10.02mm) are of similar magnitude to the slab-alone deflection (9.96mm); a more slender beam arrangement would produce a larger reduction. This confirms that the slab-only screening check used in the optimisation is a necessary but not sufficient condition: any section passing the screening check warrants a full bay-level check at the detailed design stage, particularly at longer spans where beam flexibility becomes more significant relative to slab stiffness.

\subsection{End anchorage comparison}
\label{app:end_anchorage}
%===============================================================
As stated in Section~\ref{sec:assumptions}, end anchorage by through-deck welded headed studs is omitted from the longitudinal shear resistance throughout this study, because its benefit can only be credited for decks characterised by the partial connection ($\tau_{u,Rk}$) method and not for those characterised solely by the m--k constants, and crediting it selectively would introduce an inconsistent basis of comparison. This appendix quantifies the resistance forgone by that choice, so that the magnitude of the omitted contribution is transparent. The calculation is implemented in \texttt{end\_anchorage\_comparison.py}.

\subsubsection*{Method}
The end anchorage resistance of a single through-deck welded stud is taken as the lesser of the bearing resistance of the deck rib (EN~1994-1-1 Cl.\,9.7.4 Eq.\,9.10) and the shear resistance of the stud itself (EN~1994-1-1 Cl.\,6.6.3.1):
\begin{equation}
    P_{pb,Rd} = \min\!\left( P_{bear},\; P_{stud} \right)
    \label{eq:anchorage_min}
\end{equation}
with the bearing resistance
\begin{equation}
    P_{bear} = k_\varphi\, d_{do}\, t\, f_{yp,d}, \qquad
    k_\varphi = 1 + \frac{a}{d_{do}} \le 6.0, \qquad
    d_{do} = 1.1\, d_{shank}
    \label{eq:anchorage_bear}
\end{equation}
where $a$ is the distance from the centre of the stud to the end of the sheeting and $d_{shank}$ the stud shank diameter. With one stud per rib, the anchorage force per unit width is $N_a = (1000/l_1)\,P_{pb,Rd}$, where $l_1$ is the rib spacing. This force is added to the embossment-and-friction shear bond $N_{c,\mathrm{bond}}$ to give the enhanced longitudinal force $N_c = \min(N_{c,\mathrm{bond}} + N_a,\, N_{cf})$, which is substituted into the partial connection moment resistance of Equation~\eqref{eq:partial_mrd}.

\subsubsection*{Part~1 --- Moment capacity increase from end anchorage}

Representative slab depth: $h_c = 75$\,mm above the deck, C25/30 concrete. Spans evaluated: 2.0--6.0\,m. Results cover 183 partially-connected deck$\,\times\,$span combinations.

\centering\small
\caption{Percentage increase in composite moment resistance $M_\mathrm{Rd}$ relative to embossment-only shear bond.}
\label{tab:ea_summary}

\toprule
Approach & Min\,(\%) & Max\,(\%) & Mean\,(\%) \\
\midrule

\subsubsection*{Part~2 --- Case study: Cofrastra 60, C25/30, $L = 4$\,m}

This example uses a Cofrastra 60 deck (representative gauge: $t = 0.9$\,mm, $h_p = 60$\,mm, $A_{pe} = 1067$\,mm\textsuperscript{2}/m, $f_{ypd} = 350$\,N/mm\textsuperscript{2}, $\tau_{u,Rk} = 0.10$\,N/mm\textsuperscript{2}, rib spacing $l_1 = 101$\,mm) at $L = 4$\,m simply-supported, $h = 135$\,mm, C25/30 ($f_{cd} = 16.67$\,MPa).

\paragraph{Shear span and bond force.}
The shear span is $L_x = L/2 = 2.00$\,m. The embossment bond force per unit width is
\begin{equation}
    N_{c,\mathrm{bond}} = \frac{\tau_{u,Rk}}{\gamma_{vs}}\,b\,L_x
    = \frac{0.10}{1.25}\times 1000\times2000
    = 160.0\text{\,kN/m}
\end{equation}
giving a degree of connection $\eta = 0.428$ (full connection: $N_{cf} = 373$\,kN/m).

\paragraph{End-anchorage resistance (Eq~9.10).}
For a \o\,19\,mm stud: $d_{do} = 1.1\times19 = 20.9$\,mm, $k_\phi = 1 + 40/20.9 = 2.91$.
\begin{align}
    P_{pb,Rd} &= k_\phi\,d_{do}\,t\,f_{ypd} = 2.91\times20.9\times0.9\times350 = 18757\text{\,N/stud} \\
    N_a &= \frac{1000}{101}\times18757 = 185.7\text{\,kN/m}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Design moment and resistance.}
The ULS design moment is $M_{Ed} = 21.10$\,kNm/m. The partial-connection resistance without and with end anchorage:
\begin{align}
    M_{Rd,emb}  &= 18.61\text{\,kNm/m}\quad\Rightarrow\quad \eta_{util} = 1.134\quad \textbf{(FAIL)} \\
    M_{Rd,stud} &= 31.15\text{\,kNm/m}\quad\Rightarrow\quad \eta_{util} = 0.677\quad \textbf{(PASS)} 
\end{align}
End anchorage increases $M_{Rd}$ by 67.4\,\% at a GWP cost of 5.93\,kg\,CO\textsubscript{2}e/m\textsuperscript{2} (3.25\,kg\,steel/m\textsuperscript{2}).


\subsubsection*{Discussion}
Across the 183 partially-connected deck--span combinations, end anchorage increases the composite moment resistance by a mean of 27.3\,\% at the minimum bearing factor ($k_\varphi = 2.5$) and 40.2\,\% at the maximum ($k_\varphi = 6.0$), with the largest gains at short spans where the embossment bond mobilises only a small fraction of the full-connection force. In practical terms, however, end anchorage changes the longitudinal shear feasibility outcome for only three deck families and only at a single span each: Cofraplus~60 and Cofrastra~60 at 4m, and Cofrastra~70 at 5m). At every other span the shear bond is either already sufficient without studs or insufficient even with them, so end anchorage would not extend the feasible span range.

Section~\ref{sec:assumptions}---that end anchorage cannot be credited for m--k decks and so cannot be applied uniformly across the database---this confirms that omitting end anchorage is the appropriate choice: it affects only a handful of marginal sections, the alternative (crediting studs) carries a material GWP penalty rather than a saving, and longitudinal shear in any case rarely governs the optimal sections (Section~\ref{sec:res_gov_office}).
